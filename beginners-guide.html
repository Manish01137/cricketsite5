CRICKET BETTING 101

From zero to your first ₹500 bet — the honest beginner's guide.

By the batbhai999 Education Desk · 15 min read · Last updated 24 April 2026

If you're brand new to cricket betting, the internet is a jungle of jargon, fake tipsters and "guaranteed" telegram channels. This guide cuts through that. By the end you'll understand exactly what odds mean, which markets are sensible to start on, how to size a bet, and the three habits that separate punters who last from those who burn out in a month.

1. What "odds" actually mean

In Indian / decimal format, odds are a multiplier. If RCB are priced at 1.72 and you bet ₹1,000, a win returns ₹1,720 (your ₹1,000 stake + ₹720 profit). Higher odds = lower probability the bookie thinks the outcome will happen.

Quick mental model: divide 1 by the odds to get the implied probability.

  • Odds 1.50 → 1 / 1.50 = 67% implied probability
  • Odds 2.00 → 1 / 2.00 = 50% implied probability
  • Odds 3.00 → 1 / 3.00 = 33% implied probability
If you think RCB win 70% of the time but the market prices them at 1.72 (implying 58%), that's a value bet — even if it loses, the math is in your favour over many such bets.

2. Back, lay and exchange — explained simply

Two main betting environments exist on a modern site like batbhai999:

Sportsbook (fixed odds)

The bookmaker sets the odds. You either accept the price or pass. Simpler, slower-moving, slightly worse value than Exchange.

Exchange (peer-to-peer)

Other users post odds, and you pick the price you like. You can also lay a bet — bet against an outcome (e.g. "lay RCB" means you win if RCB don't win). Exchange offers sharper prices but takes a few minutes to learn.

Beginner advice: start on Sportsbook for your first 2 weeks. Move to Exchange once you understand market dynamics.

3. The four markets you should learn first

  1. Match winner. Pick the team you think wins. Easiest to understand, hardest to consistently profit because everyone has an opinion.
  2. Top batsman / Top bowler. Pick the highest scorer / wicket-taker for one team. Smaller field of options, more analysable than full match outcome.
  3. Session over/under. Total runs in a defined block of overs (typically 1st-innings 6-over total or 15-over total). Pitch + opening pair quality is the main signal.
  4. Toss. Coin flip. Useful only if you can read which captain prefers what — Indian captains tend to bowl first under lights ~70% of the time.

4. Bankroll — the most important section

The single biggest reason new punters lose money isn't bad picks; it's bad bet sizing. The simple rule:

Never stake more than 2% of your bankroll on a single bet.

If your total betting bankroll is ₹10,000, your max single bet is ₹200. If you're feeling especially confident about something, ₹400. Never more. This rule alone keeps 80% of beginners alive long enough to actually learn the game.

Why 2%?
Even a sharp punter with a 55% win rate will go through 8-game losing streaks. At 10% stake size, an 8-loss streak wipes 80% of your bankroll. At 2%, the same streak costs you 16% — survivable, recoverable.

5. Three rookie mistakes (and how to avoid them)

Mistake 1: chasing losses

You lose ₹1,000 on a match, so you double the stake on the next one to "win it back." This is the fastest path to going broke. Stick to your 2% rule no matter what just happened.

Mistake 2: betting on every match

You don't have to bet on every game. Some matches are clear edge for you, some aren't. The pros bet on maybe 1 in 4 matches they study. Pass when you don't see edge.

Mistake 3: paying for tipster groups

If a paid telegram channel had a real edge, they'd be betting it themselves and not selling it for ₹2,000/month. Free, transparent prediction sources (like our daily predictions) with public records are vastly more honest than paid groups.

7. The "ghost rule" — never break this

Before placing any bet, ask yourself: "If this loses, will I be tempted to chase it with a bigger bet?" If yes, halve the stake. If still yes, skip the bet entirely. The discipline of not chasing is what separates the punters who are still here in 5 years from those who quit broke in 3 months.

9. Glossary — quick reference

  • Back: bet on something to happen.
  • Lay: bet against something happening.
  • Session: total runs in a defined block of overs.
  • Fancy: mini-markets within a match (e.g. runs in next over).
  • Lambi: total individual batsman score.
  • Khada / chalu: Hindi for "open / running" — refers to live markets.
  • Cash out: close a bet early at the live price.
  • Stake: the amount you risk.
  • Odds: the multiplier on a winning stake.
  • Bankroll: the total money you have set aside for betting.
